DRAFT EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT LEGISLATIVE RESOLUTION

on the proposal for a regulation of the European Parliament and of the Council on energy statistics

(COM(2006)0850 – C6‑0035/2007 – 2007/0002(COD))

(Codecision procedure: first reading)

The European Parliament,

–   having regard to the Commission proposal to the European Parliament and the Council (COM(2006)0850)[1],

–   having regard to Article 251(2) of the EC Treaty, pursuant to which the Commission submitted the proposal to Parliament (C6‑0035/2007),

–   having regard to Article 285(1) of the EC Treaty,

–   having regard to Rule 51 of its Rules of Procedure,

–   having regard to the report of the Committee on Industry, Research and Energy (A6‑0487/2007),

1.  Approves the Commission proposal as amended;

2.  Instructs its President to forward its position to the Council and Commission.

EXPLANATORY STATEMENT

Adapting the European Union’s statistics to the challenges of the 21st century

In its January 2007 energy package, the Commission presented a proposal for a Parliament and Council regulation on energy statistics (2007/0002 COD).

The great merit of this proposal is that it requires the Member States to participate in collecting the necessary data, a sine qua non for enabling the EU institutions to fulfil their stated aim, namely to phase in a consistent European energy policy and speak with a single voice on the international scene.

But the Commission proposal has a number of shortcomings, which we shall consider below. In view of the technical complexity of the subject my amendments are based on the advice of a leading expert in the field, Mr Bertrand Château, Director of Enerdata (FR), and on the contributions by other experts consulted at a workshop on the subject.

Limits of the wording proposed

1. In their present state, the statistics produced by major international organisations such as Eurostat are structured by accounting instruments dating back to a time that was completely dominated by fossil fuels, and designed to depict the operation of the energy sector solely from the supply point of view.

Developments over the past 30 years have gradually resulted in an increasing gap between the original aim of such instruments and their ability to represent energy realities. The decisive developments have included:

· the growing importance of energy efficiency;

· the growing importance of renewable energies;

· the growing importance of concerns relating to the greenhouse effect;

· the growth of nuclear power;

· the emergence of new areas of energy production, such as biofuels.

There is an increasing risk as the years go by that distortion of our understanding and assessment of energy realities will lead to decisions that are not supported by the facts.

2. The Commission proposal is the result of work carried out in 2003, 2004 and early 2005, and largely ignores some key documents on energy issues published by the Commission itself in March 2006 (the Green Paper on energy) and January 2007 (the energy package).

The energy package, for instance, includes the idea of setting up an Energy Observatory, which is to be given a legal basis in a Commission proposal expected in September 2007. To optimise the allocation of Community resources, it will be important to properly define the interaction between this new observatory and Eurostat statistics.

Adjustments required

To act as the database required for analysing and evaluating the effect of policies as implemented, European statistics need to fulfil certain conditions.

1. Reliable data and publishing schedules – to ensure politicians have the information at the right time and in the right form

1.1. Reliability

The reliability of Eurostat data took a serious battering with the problem of Greek data at the time of adopting the euro. The importance of the energy and environment issues, not just in terms of political priorities but also in financial terms (such as the value of CO2 emission certificates), makes it essential for the new regulation to provide protection from data manipulation. For this reason Article 6 needs clarifying, if not tightening up[1].

1.2. Publication schedules

A central problem of the present system is the substantial delay in the publication of Eurostat’s energy data. While the main Member States and specialist companies publish their data in six to eight months, it generally takes 18 months to obtain Eurostat statistics. This time-lag not only holds up any analysis of European policies but also affects the EU’s credibility at international level.

The delay is mainly due to a small number of Member States responding too late, or too unclearly.

It seems to us worthwhile to set closer deadlines for replies to questionnaires and a deadline for publishing Eurostat statistics.

2. The content and the degree of detail should be calibrated in accordance with the two aims of energy statistics:

· to identify energy flows, which is essential for managing the energy sector;

· to evaluate performance of the energy system in the light of energy policy objectives.

The lack of clarity of some data makes the business of reading energy statistics, and hence the lessons that can be drawn from them, exceedingly hit-and-miss. We have identified two areas where serious work is needed.

2.1. Improving the statistics for energy efficiency

The efficiency of the energy system has become a priority for all policies concerned with energy and the fight against climate change throughout Europe. Unfortunately the present statistics do not reflect that change of priorities.

The inadequacy, if not absence, of detailed statistics on final consumption by major sectors other than industry is generally preventing us from measuring the relevant ratios of technical efficiency; the difference between the degree of accuracy of information on the ‘industrial’ sector and the 70 to 80 % making up the rest of consumption (transport, residential, tertiary), which are only presented in overall aggregates, is glaringly obvious.

In the case of transport you cannot even distinguish between road passenger transport and road haulage; how are we to measure the effects of the action taken?

Our proposals for improving understanding of final energy at the lowest possible institutional and financial cost are centred on two main ideas:

· adding the detail of the main uses of final energies in the annual questionnaires, per type of energy. For instance, for the residential and tertiary sectors, there should be an obligatory breakdown for all final energy products except electricity between heating and other uses;

· conducting surveys, every three or four years, to ascertain, and then update, breakdown keys for final energies by sector and main energy uses.

2.2 Insufficient and inadequate accounting of primary energies

At the moment our understanding of energy flows depends on two requirements: the availability of information on the calorific content of the energies accounted, and the existence of instruments for measuring the physical flows.

The development of nuclear power and renewable energies is posing a number of problems in this area that have been poorly resolved, if at all.

Nuclear power

Nuclear power is at present accounted only in the case of the electricity produced by power plants. And this accounting is based not on the thermal equivalence of the electricity per Joule effect (as for the final electricity consumed), but on the heat produced by a plant of the current dominant technology, in other words with an average efficiency of around 33 %. This approach does not mention the real thermal flows from nuclear fuels, unlike the approach for coal or oil.

The other problem arising at the moment in the case of nuclear power is the limited level of specific knowledge of the energy flows involved in the fuel cycle.

To rectify this serious gap in the EU’s energy statistics, we propose adding a questionnaire on nuclear power to Annex B (annual questionnaires).

Renewables

When renewable energy flows are accounted, this is done in a number of ways:

· in the case of biomass and its derivates, on the basis of the calorific content, as for fossil fuels;

· in the case of electricity produced by hydro, wind or solar power, on the basis of kWh measured in terms of the thermal equivalent per Joule effect;

· in the case of thermal solar in direct use, on the basis of the m2 of collectors installed and the Joule/m2 ratios;

· in the case of geothermal energy, on the basis of the calorific content of the geothermal water or steam.

This method of assessing renewable energies raises the problem of the lack of knowledge of the real energy flows (particularly in the case of solar power) and the energy flows mobilised in conversions (in particular with biofuels).

To remedy these shortcomings we propose extending the data required for the biomass and solar energy sector. To prevent distortion caused by the conventions for converting final energy into primary energy, we propose basing the EU’s future objectives and statistics for renewable energies on final energy percentages (see also Chapter 3 – Indicators).

3. Indicators

Now that the EU is setting itself precise targets for energy policy, it needs the instruments for quantitative monitoring of the results: that is the role of the indicators that must determine the content and organisation of the statistical system.

3.1. Bias of statistics expressed in ‘primary energy’

The most widely used reference for depicting the energy systems of the Member States or the European Union are figures or graphs showing the share of the different energies in primary energy.

These figures and graphs are based on conversion factors between final energy and primary energy which pose a problem. At the moment, by convention, primary electricities of renewable origin are accounted on their thermal equivalent per Joule effect (1 TWh = 0.086 Mtoe), whereas electricity of nuclear origin, which is also considered as a primary electricity, is accounted on the basis of the heat produced in the reactor (1 TWh = 0.26 Mtoe by convention). Thus the same production of final electricity in TWh has a different weight in the total for primary energy production (expressed in Mtoe) depending on its origin: 1 TWh of nuclear counting for 3 times more than 1 TWh of renewable.

This has repercussions for the importance that the statistics attach to the various types of energy: they artificially inflate those that have the lowest electrical efficiency (nuclear and coal) and underestimate the share of energies with a high electrical efficiency (renewables and gas).

Bernard Laponche has made a detailed analysis of statistical documentation based on the Commission Green Paper[2] showing the extent of this distortion.

To rectify this serious distortion in interpretation of the statistics, we propose that

· the figured targets for European energy policy (improving energy efficiency, the share of renewable energies, and so on) should be expressed in terms of final energy;

· an independent group of experts should be set up under Eurostat to review the relevance of the current conversion factors.

3.2. Inconsistencies in the energy independence indicators

The way in which energy flows are accounted has a direct impact on the measurement of primary production and primary consumption, and thus on the way independence and self-sufficiency indicators are calculated. Two major sources of bias should be mentioned here:

· the absence of accounting, or only partial accounting, of some energy flows. This problem is specific to nuclear power: the fact that fissile fuels are not accounted means that they appear neither in imports nor exports, creating a de facto distortion in the evaluation of the ratios measuring independence or self-sufficiency;

· distortion in the accounting of primary energies intended for the production of electricity. The impact here, as mentioned earlier, is on our understanding of energy flows.

3.3 Misleading impressions from some energy efficiency indicators

This notion in fact refers to two separate but closely linked realities:

· the technical efficiency with which we produce and consume energy,

· the economic efficiency of the energy system.

Technical efficiency measures the ability to fulfil demand (for heat, transport, production processes and so on) with smaller quantities of energy products. It is measured by sector and by use.

Economic efficiency measures the economy’s ability to produce one euro of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) with a smaller quantity of energy (or one euro more with the same energy): it is generally measured globally by the (primary or final) energy intensity of GDP and the energy intensity of GDP at constant structure[3], which looks at total final and primary consumption.

Thus two hazards absolutely must be avoided:

· confusion between energy efficiency (in terms of energy policy targets) and structural trends in the economy[4];

· forms of bias caused by accounting primary electricity in the indicators: final energy indicators do not cause this type of problem, even if they do not make it possible to apprehend the transformation sector.

To provide a more objective reading of energy statistics, we propose that all Eurostat publications should include the following indices:

Independence and security of supply

Primary mix diversity index (Shannon-Wiener)

Independence level (primary production / primary consumption)

Overall level of self-sufficiency

Self-sufficiency level of electricity system

Energy efficiency

Primary/ final energy intensity of GDP

Primary/ final energy intensity of constant-structure GDP

ODEX

Energy per inhabitant: primary, final, electrical

Environment, climate

CO2 energy emissions

CO2 energy intensity of GDP, CO2 energy emissions per inhabitant

Share of renewable electricity in domestic electricity consumption

Share of renewables in final consumption
