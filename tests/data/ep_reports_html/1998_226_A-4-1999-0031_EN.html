REPORT on I. the proposal for a Council Directive amending Council Directive 92/106/EEC on the establishment of common rules for certain types of combined transport of goods between Member States (COM(98)0414 - C4-0485/98 - 98/0226(SYN)) II. the proposal for a Council Directive amending Council Directive 96/53/EC, laying down for certain road vehicles circulating within the Community the maximum authorised dimensions in national and international traffic and the maximum authorised weights in international traffic (COM(98)0414 - C4-0486/98 - 98/0227(SYN))

28 January 1999

Committee on Transport and Tourism
Rapporteur: Rijk van Dam

  • By letter of 6 August 1998 the Council consulted Parliament, pursuant to Article 189c of the EC Treaty and Articles 75 and 84(2) of the EC Treaty, on the proposals for Council Directives

By letter of 6 August 1998 the Council consulted Parliament, pursuant to Article 189c of the EC Treaty and Articles 75 and 84(2) of the EC Treaty, on the proposals for Council Directives

I. amending Council Directive 92/106/EEC on the establishment of common rules for certain types of combined transport of goods between Member States; and

II. amending Council Directive 96/53/EC, laying down for certain road vehicles circulating within the Community the maximum authorised dimensions in national and international traffic and the maximum authorised weights in international traffic

At the sitting of 14 September 1998 the President of Parliament announced that he had referred these proposals to the Committee on Transport and Tourism as the committee responsible and the Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs and Industrial Policy and the Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Consumer Protection for their opinions.

At its meeting of

27 October 1998 the Committee on Transport and Tourism appointed Mr Wijsenbeek rapporteur. On 20 January 1999 MrWijsenbeek resigned as rapporteur and Mr van Dam was appointed in his place.

It considered the Commission proposals and the draft report at its meetings of 27 October 1998, 23 November 1998 and 20 January 1999.

At the last meeting it adopted the draft legislative resolutions unanimously.

The following were present for the vote: Bazin, chairman; Wijsenbeek and Sisó Cruellas, vicechairmen; van Dam, rapporteur; Aparício Sánchez, Baldarelli, Bennasar Tous (for Ferri), Camisón Asensio, Castricum, Danesin, Donnay, Farassino (for Formentini), Ferber (for Koch), Grosch, Jarzembowski, Klironomos, Lagendijk, Lataillade (for Killilea), McIntosh, McMahon (for Simpson), Megahy, Moreau, Morris (for Seal), Piecyk, Rauti (for Le Rachinel), Sarlis, Schierhuber (for Langenhagen), Schlechter, Schmidbauer, Sindal, Stenmarck, Stockmann (for Swoboda) and Watts.

The Committee on Economic and Monetary Affairs and Industrial Policy decided on 28 October 1998, and the Committee on the Environment, Public Health and Consumer Protection decided on 22 September 1998 not to deliver opinions.

The report was tabled on 28 January 1999.

The deadline for tabling amendments will be indicated in the draft agenda for the relevant partsession.

B. EXPLANATORY STATEMENT

INTRODUCTION

The Commission is proposing to amend directives 92/106/EEC and 96/53/EC.

The main amendments to Directive 92/106/EC are as follows:

(a) Definition of combined transport

- the definition of combined transport will now include combined transport within a country and transport operations between a Member State and a non-Member State;

- road transport combined with short-sea transport will be regarded as combined transport but not where deep-sea shipment or short-distance ferry crossings are involved;

- the initial or final road leg is no longer part of the definition as the road section is restricted in the new proposal to 20% of the total distance of the journey.

(b) Tax rebates

- the procedures for reducing or reimbursing taxes, including the Eurovignette (Article 6(3)), are laid down in the new paragraph 1 of Article 6. These procedures have been devised to ensure that combined transport becomes more attractive even for relatively short distances as the tax rebates may relate to the transshipment costs. These costs are a sizeable proportion of the overall cost in the case of shorter distance combined transport operations.

(c) Removal of prohibitions

Goods vehicles engaged in combined transport operations will be exempted from restrictions on movement at night, weekends, and public holidays. In some countries this arrangement is already in force. With the change now proposed these arrangements are to be introduced at EU level with a view to harmonising the combined transport market. This proposal supplements the proposal recently submitted for a transparent system of harmonised rules for driving restrictions on heavy goods vehicles involved in international transport on designated roads (COM(98)0115)

- Aparício Sánchez Report)[1].

(d) Amendment of Directive 96/53/EC

This proposal would increase to 44 tonnes the weight of road vehicles engaged in combined transport operations. This would put transport on an equal footing with deep-sea transport. Some containers and tank units will therefore be able to be operated to full capacity thus reducing cost per tonne and short road legs.

Remarks and conclusions

1. Parliament has always favoured combined and intermodal transport, as witness the Tamino[2] and Stockmann[3] reports which it recently adopted. The objective of this proposal is consonant with the promotion of sustainable transport and action against road congestion.

2. The rapporteur should in principle be able to endorse the two legislative proposals, but with some reservations as regards the definition of combined transport. The use of a percentage would appear to be too constricting and rigid in for example the remoter regions where distances to transhipment terminals or loading/uploading points are considerable. To meet this objection, and resolve any problems over interpretation (20% of what distance? Can both legs of 20% added together?) of the proposal, the rapporteur is therefore proposing a more practical and realistic definition which will make it easier for the supervisory authorities and carriers to ensure that the rules are respected.

3. Another new element inserted by the amendment is the road sections which are not part of the concept which, in the strict sense of the word, are not part of the initial or final leg (i.e. transport between loading and unloading points and terminals) but which include any road legs needed for lorries to proceed to a loading point or to return from an unloading point.

4. The rapporteur considers that increasing the authorised weight to 44 tons is justified as it will place the combined transport sector on an equal footing with other sectors. At present legislation already makes an exception for ISO 40 foot containers which are mainly used in deep-sea trades.

By making the 44 ton principle generally applicable, 20 foot and 7 metre tank units as well as

13.6 metre swap bodies which are important market segments can be operated at full capacity.
